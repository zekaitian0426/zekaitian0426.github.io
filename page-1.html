About the Film

Rashomon (Japanese: 羅生門, Hepburn: Rashōmon) is a 1950 Jidaigeki drama film directed and written by Akira Kurosawa, Starring Toshiro Mifune, Machiko Kyō, and Takashi Shimura as various people who describe how a samurai was murdered in a forest, the plot and characters are based upon Ryunosuke Akutagawa's short story "In a Grove", with the title and framing story being based on "Rashōmon", another short story by Akutagawa. Every element is largely identical, from the murdered samurai speaking through a Shinto psychic to the bandit in the forest, the monk, the assault of the wife and the dishonest retelling of the events in which everyone shows their ideal self by lying.

Throuhout the film, regarding the death of the samurai, four different versions of stories are told from the bandit, the woman, the samurai, and the woodcutter.

  • Story #1
    The bandit forced the woman, but the woman eventually accepted. The woman demanded a combat between the two men, and fled away during the fight. Samurai was killed by the bandit. The bandit respected the samurai.
  • Story #2
    The woman was raped by the bandit. She freed the samurai, but the samurai, instead of gratitude, demeaned her for losing her dignitiy and not clean. The woman couldn't stand it, wanting him to kill her, but he still didn't move, just looking down on her. She couldn't take it anymore, and blacked out.
  • Story #3
    After the rape, samurai's wife decided to go with the bandit, and wanted the bandit to kill the samurai. Shocked by it, the bandit abandoned the wife, leaving her to the samurai (samurai forgave his guilt since then). The woman fled away. The bandit returned to free the samurai since he knew he will be soon captured by the police. The samurai, being so shamed, suicided using his wife's dagger.
  • Story #4
    After the rape, the samurai begged the woman to be his wife, but the wife demands a combat. But the samurai not even wanted to fight, since such a woman is not what he wanted. The wife then verbally attack the two men for being cowards. They then forced to fight, and the samurai was killed.

About the Director

Japanese director Akira Kurosawa can be said to be one of the most important directors in film history. He is mostly known for his artistic camera movements, and his unique narration styles -- they are all reflected in Rashomon very well. His camera movements can be sumamrized as having following charactersitics:

  • Movement of nature. He likes to cover scenic movements, like winds, raindrops, snowflakes, tree leaves, etc.
  • Movement of groups. He likes to include multiple people in one shot, and to show the movements of crowds. For example, group of people turning heads toward the same direction.
  • Unbroken take. He prefers master shots and Oner over frequent editings. Each of his shots have a clear beggining, middle and end.
